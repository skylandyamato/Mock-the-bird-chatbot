To Kill a Mockingbird - Chapter Summaries

  • Chapter 1

    The narrator, Scout Finch, begins by recounting the events leading up to her brother Jem's broken arm. She then introduces their small town of Maycomb, Alabama, and their mysterious neighbor, Boo Radley, who fascinates the children. Their father, Atticus Finch, a lawyer, is a central figure in their lives. The chapter sets the scene and introduces the key characters and the prevailing curiosity surrounding the Radley Place.

  • Chapter 2

    Scout starts first grade and is unimpressed with the rigid and somewhat illogical teaching methods of Miss Caroline Fisher. Scout's ability to read and write, learned from Atticus and Calpurnia (their black housekeeper), is frowned upon. The chapter highlights the social and educational disparities present in Maycomb.

  • Chapter 3

    Scout gets into trouble for explaining Walter Cunningham's poverty to Miss Caroline. Jem invites Walter home for lunch, where Scout is rude to him. Calpurnia reprimands Scout and teaches her about manners. Later, Atticus explains the Cunningham's pride and their unique situation. The chapter explores themes of social class and empathy.

  • Chapter 4

    Summer arrives, and Dill Harris returns to Maycomb. The children's fascination with Boo Radley intensifies, and they devise plans to lure him out of his house. They find small treasures in a knot-hole of an oak tree in the Radley's yard, sparking their curiosity further.

  • Chapter 5

    Atticus catches the children trying to send a note to Boo Radley and tells them to leave him alone. Scout spends more time with Miss Maudie Atkinson, their neighbor, who shares her understanding of Atticus and the Radley family. Miss Maudie clarifies some of the rumors surrounding Boo.

  • Chapter 6

    The children attempt to peek into the Radley house at night but are startled by a shotgun blast. Jem loses his pants while escaping, and later retrieves them to avoid Atticus's suspicion. The chapter builds suspense and highlights the children's reckless curiosity.

  • Chapter 7

    Jem tells Scout about finding his pants mended and folded on the fence when he went back for them. The children continue to find small gifts in the knot-hole, suggesting Boo Radley's quiet attempts at connection. They are saddened when Nathan Radley fills the hole with cement.

  • Chapter 8

    Maycomb experiences an unusual snowfall. Miss Maudie's house catches fire, and the community comes together to help. Scout is mysteriously wrapped in a blanket during the fire, and Jem realizes it must have been Boo Radley, showing a moment of unexpected kindness.

  • Chapter 9

    Atticus agrees to defend Tom Robinson, a black man accused of assaulting a white woman. Scout faces taunts and prejudice from her classmates because of her father's decision. Atticus explains his moral obligation to take the case, emphasizing the importance of justice and integrity.

  • Chapter 10

    The children are ashamed of Atticus's age and perceived lack of interesting skills until he calmly shoots a mad dog, Tim Johnson, demonstrating his hidden talent and courage. Miss Maudie explains Atticus's past as "One-Shot Finch," revealing a new dimension to his character.

  • Chapter 11

    The children encounter the cantankerous Mrs. Henry Lafayette Dubose, who constantly insults Atticus. Jem loses his temper and destroys her camellia bushes. As punishment, he must read to her daily. Through this experience, the children learn about courage and resilience in the face of adversity.

  • Chapter 12

    Dill does not come to Maycomb for the summer. Jem begins to mature and distance himself from Scout. Calpurnia takes the children to her black church, where they experience a different side of Maycomb's social structure and face some prejudice.

  • Chapter 13

    Aunt Alexandra comes to live with the Finches to provide a feminine influence for Scout and to instill family pride. Her traditional views clash with Atticus's more progressive parenting style and Scout's tomboyish nature.

  • Chapter 14

    Scout and Aunt Alexandra clash frequently. Scout misses Dill and is confused by the growing tension in the house. Jem reveals that Dill has run away from home and is hiding under Scout's bed. The children try to understand Dill's family situation.

  • Chapter 15

    Rumors and prejudice surrounding the upcoming trial intensify. A group of men comes to the Finch house to confront Atticus about defending Tom Robinson. Jem, Scout, and Dill witness this tense encounter, and Atticus calmly faces the threat.

  • Chapter 16

    The day of the trial arrives, and the atmosphere in Maycomb is charged. The children sneak into the courtroom and observe the proceedings from the balcony with the black community. The chapter sets the stage for the dramatic testimony to come.

  • Chapter 17

    The prosecution presents its case, focusing on the testimony of Sheriff Heck Tate and the alleged victim, Mayella Ewell. Tate's testimony reveals inconsistencies. Mayella appears uneducated, lonely, and seemingly afraid of her father.

  • Chapter 18

    Mayella Ewell gives her emotional and contradictory testimony, accusing Tom Robinson of assault. Atticus's cross-examination reveals her difficult home life, her possible attraction to Tom, and the inconsistencies in her story, suggesting her father might be responsible for her injuries.

  • Chapter 19

    Tom Robinson gives his honest and straightforward testimony, explaining his regular assistance to Mayella and denying the assault. He reveals that Mayella often initiated contact and that her father caught them together. His sincerity contrasts sharply with the Ewells' testimonies.

  • Chapter 20

    During Atticus's closing argument, he eloquently appeals to the jury's sense of justice and reason, highlighting the lack of credible evidence against Tom and the ingrained racial prejudice at play. Scout and Dill are taken outside by Jem when he senses Atticus's emotional intensity.

  • Chapter 21

    The jury deliberates for a long time. Finally, they return a guilty verdict. The black community in the balcony shows their respect for Atticus as he leaves the courtroom. The children are deeply affected by the injustice.

  • Chapter 22

    The black community shows their gratitude to Atticus by sending him food. Miss Maudie explains the small steps of progress being made and defends Atticus's efforts. Bob Ewell confronts Atticus, spitting in his face and threatening revenge.

  • Chapter 23

    Atticus calmly dismisses Bob Ewell's threats. He explains to Jem and Scout that Ewell needed to vent his anger. The children discuss the injustice of the verdict and the complexities of prejudice. Atticus reveals that one juror initially held out for acquittal.

  • Chapter 24

    Aunt Alexandra hosts a missionary circle meeting at the Finch house. The ladies of Maycomb display hypocritical attitudes, expressing concern for far-off people while remaining prejudiced against their own black community. News arrives that Tom Robinson was shot and killed while trying to escape prison.

  • Chapter 25

    The town reacts to Tom's death with varying degrees of interest and gossip. An editorial in the Maycomb Tribune condemns the injustice of his death. Scout reflects on the events of the summer and begins to understand the deep-seated prejudice in her community.

  • Chapter 26

    Life in Maycomb returns to a semblance of normalcy, though the events of the trial and Tom's death have left a lasting impact. Scout starts third grade and learns about Hitler in class, highlighting the irony of prejudice at home.

  • Chapter 27

    Bob Ewell continues to harbor a grudge and makes veiled threats against Atticus and others involved in the trial. There are three seemingly unrelated incidents: Ewell tries to break into Judge Taylor's house, harasses Helen Robinson, and follows the Finch children.

  • Chapter 28

    On their way home from the school Halloween pageant, Jem and Scout are attacked in the dark. Jem is injured, and Scout is saved by a mysterious figure. The attacker is revealed to be Bob Ewell.

  • Chapter 29

    Sheriff Tate investigates the attack and finds Bob Ewell dead, stabbed with a kitchen knife. Atticus initially believes Jem was responsible, but Tate insists that Ewell fell on his own knife, protecting Boo Radley.

  • Chapter 30

    Boo Radley is brought into the Finch house. Atticus and Sheriff Tate discuss the circumstances of Bob Ewell's death. Tate convinces Atticus that revealing Boo's heroism would subject him to unwanted attention, and they agree to report Ewell's death as accidental.

  • Chapter 31

    Scout finally meets and talks with Boo Radley. She leads him home, imagining the world from his perspective as she stands on his porch. Atticus reads to her, and she falls asleep, understanding the events of the past few years and the true nature of courage and prejudice.
